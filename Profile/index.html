Samut Prakan

The province was created during the era of the Ayutthaya Kingdom, with its administrative centre at Prapadaeng. It was the sea port of Siam, and was secured with forts, town moats, and town walls. King Rama II started building the new centre at Samut Prakan in 1819, after his predecessor King Taksin had abandoned the town fortifications. Altogether six forts were built on both sides of the Chao Phraya River, and on an island in the river the pagoda, Phra Samut Chedi, was erected. These were involved in the Paknam incident of 13 July 1893, which ended the Franco-Siamese War with a French naval blockade of Bangkok. Of the original six forts only two still exist today, Phi Sua Samut and Phra Chulachomklao. In 1942, there was an act that included Phra Nakhon, Thonburi, Samut Prakan and Nonthaburi together, and in 1943 there is a new government regulation collapsed Samut Prakan with Phra Nakhon. On May 9, 1946, Samut Prakan was separated from Phra Nakhon until today
